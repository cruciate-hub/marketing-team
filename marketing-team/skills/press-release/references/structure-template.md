# Press Release Structure Template

This is the canonical skeleton. Every social.plus press release follows it. Block-by-block specifications below.

## The 12-block skeleton

```
[1]  FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE
[2]  Headline
[3]  Subhead
[4]  Dateline + lede paragraph
[5]  Second paragraph (context / what's included)
[6]  Section subhead — the problem / why now
[7]  2–4 paragraphs framing the category gap or strategic context
[8]  Executive quote + attribution
[9]  Section subhead — what's new / what's announced
[10] Detail paragraphs (one per feature, deal point, or proof point)
[11] Customer or partner quote + attribution
[12] Section subhead — industry / positioning framing
[13] Industry-fit paragraph
[14] Availability / how to get it / next steps
[15] About social.plus boilerplate
[16] Media contact
[17] ### end marker
```

Some release types compress or expand specific blocks. See `release-type-playbooks.md`.

## Block-by-block spec

### Block 1 — FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE

Always exactly: `FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE` in caps. If embargoed, replace with `UNDER EMBARGO UNTIL [DAY], [MONTH DD, YYYY], [TIME] [TIMEZONE]`.

Left-aligned. Plain. No bold. Top of page.

### Block 2 — Headline

One line. 8–14 words. 70–110 characters including spaces.

**House pattern: colon construction.**
- `[Subject]: [Outcome or claim]`
- `[Company] [verb] [thing]: [why it matters]`

Examples that work:
- `social.plus Launches Commerce: Turning Community Into the New Storefront`
- `social.plus Raises $30M Series B to Power the Owned Community Economy`
- `Smart Fit Partners with social.plus to Bring Community to 5 Million Members`

Rules:
- Sentence case for the subject side, sentence case for the predicate side after the colon
- Active verb
- Specific noun (not "platform" — name what it is)
- No adjective inflation
- No ellipsis, no question marks, no exclamation marks

### Block 3 — Subhead

One sentence, italic, 14–25 words, 100–180 characters.

The subhead does one job: explains what the news *enables* that the headline does not say. It is the "so what" line.

Pattern: `[New capability or fact] [verbs] [outcome], [secondary clause adding scope].`

From the Commerce example:
> New capabilities bring product discovery directly into community posts and livestreams, closing the gap between high-intent conversation and action

Rules:
- No restating the headline
- No adjective stack
- No trailing period? Optional — house style omits it for the subhead, includes it everywhere else.

### Block 4 — Dateline + lede paragraph

Format: `CITY, Month DD, YYYY` followed by an em-dash, then the lede. The CITY is in caps.

Examples:
- `LONDON, April 29, 2026 — `
- `NEW YORK, October 17, 2026 — `

For non-US datelines, no state. For US, AP-style state abbreviations only if not a well-known city: `SAN FRANCISCO, CA, October 17, 2026 —` (omit "CA" for SF; include for "BOULDER, CO,").

Lede paragraph:
- One paragraph, 35–60 words
- Sentence 1: Who, What, When, Where, in that order. Always names social.plus with its descriptor on first mention.
- Sentence 2: A second-tier detail or the "why" (light context, not the deep narrative).
- Active voice. Past or present tense. Never future.

Descriptor format (first mention of social.plus):
> social.plus, the platform powering in-app social and community experiences for leading consumer brands, [verbed] [the news].

This descriptor is the canonical first-mention introduction across every release type. Do not change it inline — if it needs updating, edit `references/structure-template.md` and `references/release-type-playbooks.md` together so every lede formula stays consistent.

Variants depending on release type:
- Funding: `social.plus, the platform powering in-app social and community experiences for leading consumer brands, today announced a $X [Series Y] funding round led by [investor].`
- Partnership: `social.plus, the platform powering in-app social and community experiences for leading consumer brands, today announced a partnership with [partner] to [outcome].`
- Customer: `social.plus, the platform powering in-app social and community experiences for leading consumer brands, today announced that [customer], the [customer descriptor], has [verbed].`

### Block 5 — Second paragraph

40–80 words. Does ONE of:
- Lists what is included if multiple parts (e.g., "Commerce is now available across social.plus and includes three interconnected capabilities: ...")
- Provides one short specific number that sets scale (e.g., "Over 1,200 brands use social.plus today.")
- Names the partner/investor/customer in slightly more detail if not in the lede

Do not introduce a new claim here — only support the lede.

### Blocks 6–7 — Section subhead + 2–4 paragraphs of category/problem framing

This is where world-class B2B PR earns the read. The skill must frame *why this announcement matters in the wider industry* before getting into the product itself.

Subhead style:
- Sentence case
- 5–10 words
- A statement, not a question
- Examples: "The gap Commerce is built to close", "Why owned community matters now", "Building for the post-platform era"

Body paragraphs:
- 2–4 paragraphs
- Each 50–90 words
- Establish the category problem, the inflection point, or the customer pain
- Use one or two concrete vignettes (e.g., "A member posts about a product they love. A creator holds something up on a livestream.") — these are gold when they work
- End the section with the bridge sentence that pivots to the announcement: "[Product/news] changes this."

### Block 8 — Executive quote + attribution

One paragraph, italics or quotation marks (the .docx generator uses curly quotes, no italics, indented).

Quote rules — see `quote-engineering.md` for full guidance. Summary:
- 35–65 words, 1–2 sentences
- Makes a category-level claim, not a product-feature recitation
- Quotable on its own (a journalist can lift it without context)
- Speaker sounds like a CEO or VP, not a marketing email

Attribution format (one line, name then comma then title):
```
Amadeus Norén, VP of Marketing
```

No "said" verb if the attribution is on its own line directly below the quote (this is house style — matches the Commerce example).

### Block 9 — Section subhead — what's new

Often literally `What's new` or `What's included`. For non-launch releases, use something specific:
- Funding: `What the funding enables`
- Partnership: `What the partnership delivers`
- Hire: `What [Name] brings`

### Block 10 — Detail paragraphs

One per feature, deal point, or proof point.

Each paragraph:
- Starts with the named thing in bold-equivalent (the .docx generator bolds the first phrase)
- 50–100 words
- Names the user-facing capability and the underlying mechanism
- Mentions an outcome or proof point if available
- No adjective stacking

Pattern from the Commerce example:
> **Product Tagging in Posts** allows brands and moderators to tag catalogue items directly inside any post: text, image, video, or mixed-media. Users can engage with tagged products without leaving the platform, while every view and click is captured as structured data to measure content-to-product attribution.

For non-product releases, this block carries the *substance* of the news:
- Funding: "Use of funds" paragraph — typically R&D, GTM expansion, key hires
- Partnership: What each side brings, what customers get
- Customer win: What they built, how long it took, what changed

### Block 11 — Customer or partner quote + attribution

Same format as the executive quote. Required for product launches, partnerships, customer wins, and funding rounds with named investors. Optional for milestones, hires, and awards.

The customer/partner quote validates from outside. It must come from the brief — never drafted by the skill. If the brief omits it for a release type that requires it, ask the user before drafting.

### Block 12 — Section subhead — industry framing

Standard subheads:
- `Built for every industry, not just [primary vertical]`
- `Designed for [audience], used across [industries]`
- `Why this matters for [audience]`

Pattern: position the announcement against the next-largest credible market or use case.

### Block 13 — Industry-fit paragraph

50–100 words. Names 2–4 specific verticals or use cases with one-clause examples. Closes with a one-sentence reframing of the announcement's strategic value.

### Block 14 — Availability / next steps

40–80 words. Three components:
- When it's available (now / on [date] / in [quarter])
- Who can get it / how (existing customers via Console / contact sales / sign up at URL)
- Where to read documentation (learn.social.plus or docs URL)

For non-product releases:
- Funding: who the investors are (named in the block), board changes if relevant
- Partnership: contract terms only if cleared, otherwise availability of joint offering
- Hire: start date, reporting line

### Block 15 — About social.plus boilerplate

Always titled `About social.plus`. Pulled verbatim from `boilerplates.md` (Step 1 of SKILL.md). The default is:

> social.plus is the infrastructure layer for in-app community. The platform gives brands the building blocks to create owned social experiences — connection, conversation, content, and commerce — inside their own apps, with full ownership of the data, the design, and the customer relationship. Brands across fitness, travel, retail, sports, and entertainment use social.plus to make community core to how their product works. The best version of every app is Social+.

**Why this version (don't change it lightly):**

- The four C's framing — **connection, conversation, content, and commerce** — covers everything the platform does today and the foreseeable roadmap, so the boilerplate doesn't have to be rewritten every release. Resist the urge to swap in literal feature names (activity feeds, groups, livestreams, etc.) — that ages every press release and reads like a deck.
- The differentiator is **positive ownership** ("full ownership of the data, the design, and the customer relationship") rather than defensive framing about third-party networks. Same point, stronger posture.
- The vertical list does the credibility work on its own. Avoid adding "leading brands" — it's empty.
- The tagline stays.

**When to override:**

- Use a customer-anchored variant ("Brands including [Customer 1], [Customer 2], and [Customer 3]...") only when those customers are formally approved as named references and the release type warrants it (anniversaries, funding rounds, major milestones).
- For partnership and acquisition releases, append the second company's boilerplate as Block 15b in alphabetical order.
- Do not add the announcement itself into the boilerplate ("...and now [new product]") — the body of the release carries the new news. The boilerplate is identity, not news.

### Block 16 — Media contact

Always titled `Media Contact`. One line:
```
marketing@social.plus | social.plus
```

If the brief specifies a different contact (PR firm, named comms lead), use that instead.

### Block 17 — End marker

A single line: `###`

Centered. This is the universal newswire convention for end-of-release. Some agencies use `-30-` instead; `###` is the AP/PR Newswire standard and is what we use.

## Length targets by release type

| Release type        | Word count | Notes |
|---------------------|------------|-------|
| Product launch      | 500–800    | The most common shape; full skeleton |
| Funding round       | 600–900    | Add investor section + use of funds |
| Partnership         | 400–700    | Co-quote from partner is mandatory |
| Customer win        | 400–650    | Customer voice carries the back half |
| Exec hire           | 300–500    | Compress problem framing; expand bio |
| Milestone / metric  | 350–550    | Lede leads with the number |
| Award               | 300–450    | Shortest format; one strong quote |
| Acquisition         | 600–1000   | Two boilerplates (acquirer + target) |

Anything under 300 words is a media advisory, not a press release. Anything over 1000 risks being trimmed by the wire.
