# Best-in-class B2B tech press release corpus

Distilled patterns from the press releases of B2B tech companies that consistently get covered well. Use these as conventions to apply, not as text to reproduce.

## The seven companies studied

- **Stripe** — Master of plain-English ledes and quotes that take category positions
- **Datadog** — Discipline in subhead structure; conservative claims that get believed
- **Snowflake** — Tight, founder-voiced quotes; clean inverted pyramid
- **MongoDB** — Strong customer-win releases; clear use-of-funds language in funding rounds
- **Cloudflare** — Best-in-class for product launches; technical specificity without jargon
- **HashiCorp** — Open-source-savvy releases; honest about community vs. commercial
- **Atlassian** — Clean partnership and acquisition releases; coordinated co-quotes

---

## Pattern 1 — The "plain English lede" (Stripe school)

Stripe consistently writes ledes that an intelligent generalist reader can parse in one pass. No jargon, no acronyms, no category labels.

**Template:**
> `[CITY], [Date] — [Company], [short descriptor in plain English], today announced [the thing], [one-clause description of what it does in plain English].`

The descriptor never includes phrases like "AI-powered", "next-generation", "blockchain-enabled". It says what the product *is* in the words a customer would use.

**Applied to social.plus:**
> "social.plus, the platform powering in-app social and community experiences for leading consumer brands, announced..." ✓
>
> NOT: "social.plus, the AI-powered, next-generation, blockchain-enabled community SaaS platform, announced..." ✗

---

## Pattern 2 — The "category claim" exec quote (Stripe / Snowflake school)

Founder/CEO quotes consistently make a category claim that puts a stake in the ground. The claim could be wrong; that's the point — having a position is what makes it quotable.

**Template:**
> "[Audience] used to [old behavior]. They don't anymore — [specific new behavior]. [Product/company] is the [category descriptor] for that world."

Or:
> "[Strategic condition]. Until [condition is met], [outcome] won't happen. [Product/announcement] is how we [solve for the condition]."

The Commerce example follows the second template exactly. This is the strongest pattern available.

---

## Pattern 3 — The "use of funds" specificity (MongoDB / HashiCorp school)

Funding-round press releases that get covered well always have a "use of funds" paragraph with three things, not five. The three are usually:
1. R&D (named domain, not "the platform")
2. GTM (named region or segment, not "growth")
3. Hires (named function, not "team expansion")

**Template:**
> "The funding will [accelerate / fund / support] three priorities: [specific R&D area], [specific GTM expansion — region or segment], and [specific hires — function and seniority]."

**Applied:**
> "The funding will support three priorities: deepening the Commerce capabilities announced in April, expanding go-to-market in EMEA and APAC, and hiring senior engineering leadership in London and Singapore."

---

## Pattern 4 — The "what's included" middle (Cloudflare school)

When announcing a multi-part launch, Cloudflare-style releases dedicate one detail paragraph per component. Each paragraph:
- Starts with the component name bolded (or lead-styled)
- Has 2–4 sentences
- Names one user-facing capability AND one underlying mechanism
- Closes with an outcome or proof point if available

The Commerce example follows this exactly across three components (Product Catalogue, Product Tagging in Posts, Tag and Pin in Livestream). Each paragraph is structurally identical — that's the discipline.

---

## Pattern 5 — The "industry framing" pivot (Atlassian / Datadog school)

After the product detail, strong B2B tech releases have a section that explicitly addresses *who else this is for*. The subhead is usually a statement that pivots:

- "Built for every industry, not just [primary vertical]"
- "Designed for [primary audience], used across [secondary audiences]"
- "Beyond [primary use case]"

This section serves two purposes:
1. Tells generalist journalists the broader market context
2. Plants the seed for follow-on releases (you can come back later with a vertical-specific release)

---

## Pattern 6 — The "co-quote choreography" (Atlassian partnership school)

In partnership and acquisition releases, the two quotes (one per company) must not be mirror images. Each speaker makes a different point.

- Speaker A (the announcing/acquiring company): names what the gap was on their side and how this fills it
- Speaker B (the partner/acquired): makes a forward-looking statement about what becomes possible for *their* customers/community

If both quotes essentially say "this is great, we're delighted, we're aligned" — both fail. Rewrite at least one with a specific differentiated claim.

---

## Pattern 7 — The "customer is the hero" customer-win release (MongoDB customer school)

Customer-win releases that work make the customer the protagonist. social.plus shows up as the platform they chose, not the savior.

**Template (lede):**
> "[Customer], [customer descriptor], today announced that it has [done the thing] using social.plus."

Note: the lede may be framed as the customer's announcement co-released with social.plus, or as a social.plus release that names the customer. Either works; the customer's permission and approval determines which.

The body emphasizes:
- What the customer was trying to do (their goal, not social.plus's product)
- The decision process (why they chose social.plus — implicit credibility for the platform)
- What they built (specific to the customer)
- The result (their metric, in their words)

Two quotes: customer first, social.plus second. The customer quote is the longer one. social.plus's quote in this release type plays a supporting role — it should validate the *category* (community infrastructure is a real category) without claiming credit for the customer's success.

## Pattern 8 — The "honest about scope" claim discipline (HashiCorp school)

HashiCorp's releases consistently avoid overclaiming. Patterns to emulate:

- "Used by over [N] organizations" — specific number, not "thousands"
- "Available in [region/SKU]" — names the scope, doesn't imply global availability if it isn't
- "Supports [specific list]" — enumerates, doesn't say "supports virtually every"
- "Processed [N] [unit] in the past [time period]" — bounded claim

When in doubt, be honest about the boundary. A bounded claim that's true is stronger than an unbounded claim that's vague.

---

## Pattern 9 — The "no future tense" lede

Across all seven companies, ledes are past tense or present tense. Never future.

- ✓ "social.plus today launched Commerce..."
- ✓ "social.plus announced..."
- ✗ "social.plus will launch Commerce next week..."

If something hasn't launched yet, the release date is wrong. Move the date forward and write in present/past.

The one exception: a planned future event with confirmed timing (e.g., "available in Q3 2026"). That goes in the availability block (Block 14), not the lede.

---

## Pattern 10 — The "boilerplate carries the tagline" rule

Across the corpus, the company's tagline appears in the boilerplate, never in the body or the headline. The body of the release is news; the boilerplate is identity. Mixing them dilutes both.

For social.plus, the tagline "The best version of every app is Social+" appears at the end of the About block — not in the body paragraphs, not in quotes, not in the headline.

## What NOT to take from the corpus

These are common in B2B PR but counterproductive:

- **Boilerplate Magic Quadrant / analyst recognition stuffing** — citing every analyst recognition you've ever received in the boilerplate. Cite at most one if it's truly defining; otherwise omit.
- **The "trusted by" customer logo dump** — naming 5+ customers in the lede or boilerplate. Name 2–3 max, and only ones with public reference status.
- **The vanity URL pile-up** — three URLs in a single paragraph. Limit URLs to one in availability (docs) and one in About (homepage).
- **The press contact who never responds** — generic "press@" addresses with no human routing. The Media Contact block should be reachable.
- **The acknowledgment paragraph** — "social.plus would like to thank...". This isn't an Oscar speech. Cut.

---

## When to break the patterns

The patterns are conventions, not laws. Break them when the news demands it:

- A truly singular milestone (first IPO in a category, a billion-dollar deal) may warrant a non-standard structure
- An emergency communication (security incident, customer impact) follows a different rulebook entirely — see crisis-comms playbook (not yet part of this skill)
- A creative campaign tied to a launch may use a less-conventional format if Marketing has explicitly signed off

For the default — the routine product launches, fundings, partnerships, customer wins, and milestones — the patterns above produce a release that reads like it came from a competent B2B tech PR firm. Combined with the structure template, quote engineering guide, and anti-patterns checklist, the output should be indistinguishable from a top-tier agency draft.
