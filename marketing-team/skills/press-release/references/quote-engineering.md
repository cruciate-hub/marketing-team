# Quote Engineering

Quotes are the single most-skipped, most-skimmed, and most-misused element of a press release. A well-engineered quote is what a journalist actually reads twice and decides to lift into their story. A bad quote signals the entire release isn't worth attention.

This guide covers:
1. The four-rule test every quote must pass
2. How to draft executive quotes from a speaker name + title
3. How to handle customer/partner quotes (which the skill never drafts)
4. Patterns and anti-patterns

---

## The four-rule quote test

Every quote in the release — executive, customer, partner — must pass all four:

### Rule 1: The Lift Test

If a journalist copy-pastes only this quote into their article (with no surrounding context from the release), does it still mean something specific?

A quote that fails this test is a quote that depends on the lede to make sense. Those quotes get cut.

### Rule 2: The Delete Test

If you delete this quote from the release, what factual information is lost?

If the answer is "nothing" — the quote is doing its job. Quotes add color, conviction, and a human voice. They do NOT carry facts. Facts go in the body paragraphs.

If the answer is "the announcement makes less sense" — the quote is carrying load it shouldn't carry. Move the fact to a body paragraph.

### Rule 3: The Sounds-Like-a-Human Test

Read the quote out loud. Does it sound like something a real person would say in a coffee meeting?

If it sounds like a marketing email, rewrite. Specific markers of "marketing email" voice:
- "We are excited to..."
- "We are pleased to..."
- "thrilled", "delighted", "honored"
- Stacked superlatives ("our best-in-class, industry-leading solution")
- Adjective-heavy sentences with no verb of substance

### Rule 4: The Category Test

Does the quote make a claim about the *category*, *industry*, *user behavior*, or *strategic shift* — not just the product?

The strongest quotes zoom out. They take a position. They commit the speaker to a worldview that other people might disagree with. That's why they get quoted.

If the quote is essentially "[product] is great because it does X" — rewrite. Find the category claim that justifies why X matters.

## Drafting executive quotes (the skill writes these)

The brief provides the speaker's full name and title. The skill drafts the quote for the speaker's review.

### Step 1 — Identify the speaker's lens

What does this speaker uniquely see? CEO sees the category and the customer landscape. CTO sees what becomes technically possible. CPO sees user behavior shifts. VP of Marketing sees how the conversation in the industry is changing. CFO sees economic dynamics.

Match the quote's claim to the speaker's natural vantage point. Don't put a category claim in a CTO's mouth or a technical mechanism in a CMO's.

### Step 2 — Write the category claim sentence

Start with the second sentence first. The category claim. The thing that, if true, makes this announcement matter.

Template patterns that work:

> "Until [category-level condition is met], [strategic outcome] remains [adjective]."

(From the Commerce example: "Until commerce is a core part of the community experience, justifying investment in community remains challenging.")

> "[Audience] don't want [old model]. They want [new model]."

> "The biggest [audience] aren't [old behavior] anymore. They're [new behavior]."

> "[Category] has changed. [Specific shift]. And [old assumption] no longer holds."

> "[Adjective negative state] isn't a [audience] problem. It's an infrastructure problem."

### Step 3 — Write the bridge sentence

Now write sentence 1 (or merge with the category claim if a single sentence is enough).

The bridge connects the category claim to *this* announcement. Patterns:

> "[Product/news] [verbs] [specific outcome]: [mechanism in plain language]."

(From Commerce: "Commerce gives our customers the infrastructure to close that loop: not by bolting on a shop, but by making every high-intent moment inside the community actionable.")

> "[Announcement] is how we [do the thing the category needs done]."

> "We built [thing] because [audience] kept asking us for [outcome] — and we realized [insight]."

### Step 4 — Tighten

Cut every word that isn't doing work. Goal: 35–65 words total, 1–2 sentences. Two short sentences are better than one long one.

### Step 5 — Voice-check by role

For a final pass, re-read the quote AS the named speaker:

- CEO Eric (or whoever): Does the quote commit the company to a worldview? Is it a position they would defend in an interview?
- VP Marketing: Is the language about the market and the audience, not the product features?
- CTO: Is the claim about what becomes possible technically? Are the verbs precise?

If the speaker would push back ("I wouldn't say that") — rewrite. The drafted quote exists for the speaker's review and approval; it should be 90% of the way there.

## Customer and partner quotes (always from the brief)

The skill **never drafts** customer or partner quotes. Three reasons:

1. **Legal:** Putting words in someone else's mouth without their approval is fabrication. It exposes the company.
2. **Trust:** Customers and partners discover their "own" quotes when the release publishes. Trust evaporates instantly.
3. **Quality:** Real customer language is better than anything a writer invents — it has the specificity and the texture that drafted quotes don't.

If the brief omits a customer/partner quote for a release type that requires one (partnership, customer win), pause and ask the user before drafting.

When the brief provides a customer quote:
- Use the text verbatim. Do not "edit" except to fix obvious typos
- If the quote is too long (over 80 words), ask the user which portion to use — don't trim unilaterally
- If the quote fails the four-rule test, flag it back to the user ("This quote doesn't stand alone — want me to ask [customer] for a stronger one?")
- Confirm attribution: full name, exact title, exact company name

## Patterns from the corpus

### From the Commerce example (gold standard)

> "Until commerce is a core part of the community experience, justifying investment in community remains challenging. Commerce gives our customers the infrastructure to close that loop: not by bolting on a shop, but by making every high-intent moment inside the community actionable."
>
> Amadeus Norén, VP of Marketing

Why this works:
- **Lift test:** Stands alone — a journalist could quote it in any "community commerce" article without needing context
- **Delete test:** No facts in the quote — the lede and body carry the news
- **Sounds-like-a-human test:** No "excited", no "pleased", no superlatives
- **Category test:** First sentence is a category claim ("Until commerce is a core part of the community experience..."). The second sentence positions the product within that worldview.

### From the broader B2B tech corpus

**Stripe (Stripe Capital launch):**
> "Most online businesses still can't get a loan. That's not because they aren't creditworthy — it's because the system was built for a world without internet businesses."

Why it works: Rule 4 (category claim about how the financial system works). No mention of Stripe Capital's features.

**Datadog (acquisition announcement, paraphrased pattern):**
> "Observability used to be about reactive debugging. It's becoming the operating system of every cloud business — and [Acquired Company] is the most important team thinking about [niche capability]."

Why it works: Stakes out a position on what observability is becoming. The acquired company appears in the second clause as a consequence of that position.

**Snowflake (early Snowpipe announcement, paraphrased pattern):**
> "Customers told us they didn't want to choose between real-time and cost-effective. So we stopped accepting it as a trade-off."

Why it works: Rule 3 (sounds like a human). Implies a category critique ("everyone else accepts the trade-off") without naming competitors.

## The banned-quote anti-pattern catalog

If a draft quote matches any of these patterns, rewrite.

### Anti-pattern 1: The summary quote
> "We're excited to launch Commerce, a new suite of capabilities that brings product discovery into posts and livestreams. This is a major step forward for the social.plus platform."

Why it fails: Summarizes what the lede already said. Delete test fails (the quote restates facts). Rule 4 fails (no category claim).

### Anti-pattern 2: The gratitude quote
> "We're honored to partner with [Customer]. Their team is incredible and we couldn't have asked for a better customer to work with."

Why it fails: Sounds like a marketing email. Says nothing about the work itself. Rule 3 + Rule 4 both fail.

### Anti-pattern 3: The adjective stack
> "Our cutting-edge, industry-leading platform is delivering best-in-class outcomes that are revolutionizing the community space."

Why it fails: Every adjective is replaceable. No specific claim. Rule 1 and Rule 4 fail.

### Anti-pattern 4: The "as we" quote
> "As we continue to expand our platform, we're committed to delivering more value to our customers across all industries."

Why it fails: Says nothing about the announcement. Could appear in any release the company has ever issued. Rule 2 (delete) fails completely.

### Anti-pattern 5: The CEO playing CTO (or vice versa)
> CEO quote: "Our new event-driven architecture leverages a distributed consensus protocol to ensure consistent state across nodes."

Why it fails: Wrong vantage point. CEO speaks to category and customer. CTOs talk architecture. Trust collapses when the speaker's voice doesn't match their role.

### Anti-pattern 6: The strawman quote
> "Many community platforms claim to support commerce, but they don't really mean it."

Why it fails: Vague swipe at unnamed competitors. Sounds defensive. Use specifics or skip the contrast.

## Final checklist before a quote ships

Before the draft release moves to .docx generation, verify each quote:

1. ⬜ Passes the four-rule test
2. ⬜ 35–65 words
3. ⬜ 1–2 sentences
4. ⬜ Speaker's vantage point matches their role
5. ⬜ No banned adjectives ("excited", "thrilled", "honored", "delighted")
6. ⬜ No "as we" / "we are pleased" / "we are excited" openings
7. ⬜ Customer/partner quotes are verbatim from the brief
8. ⬜ Attribution: full name + exact title (no nicknames)

A release with two great quotes lands. A release with two filler quotes is a release a journalist scrolls past.
